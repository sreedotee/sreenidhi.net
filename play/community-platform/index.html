Panic! at the Party

Community apps are exploding right now. Everyone is either building one or living on three. I wanted to understand what actually happens at scale by building one and watching it run. This was one of those experiments.

I went down a UXR rabbit hole recently and came across this UX Design piece on using simulation models in user research. The idea of watching a whole ecosystem play out instead of testing one variable at a time stuck with me, so I wanted to try agent-based modelling for myself. Build a tiny synthetic platform, turn some knobs, see what happens.

The model is a city-scale Luma. About 100 users move through six states: new, passive, active, host, inactive, churned. Each carries a topic interest (drawn from a power-law, so lots of mainstream and few niche), a social motive (homebody to butterfly), and a budget. Events spawn daily. Some are host-created in the host's own niche, some are platform-curated round-robin across topics, and they live a day or two the way real events do. Users find events by proximity, topic match, motive, and affordability. Hosts emerge from sustained attendance, then keep going only as long as their events draw people. Burnout is built in.

What to watch in the snapshots and the live model below: yellow stars are events. Person dots are users. Sky blue (new), gray (passive), green (active), orange (host), dark blue (inactive), red (churned). Orange is always the smallest population, and the most load-bearing.

Day 50, healthy parameters. One orange host has just emerged. Most of the population is still passive (gray) or churning out (red). One event running. The platform is alive but hasn't caught fire yet.

Day 110, same parameters. A dense host cluster has formed in one topic, drawing event stars and a green ring of regular attendees. This is what "alive" looks like: concentrated, spatially clustered, sustained by a small group of hosts.

Day 220, adverse parameters (low event creation, fast satisfaction decay, no notifications). The platform never built a host class. With no orange dots seeding events, the rest unravels. Heavy red churn, scattered survivors. Same model, different knobs, completely different fate.

A few things kept showing up across runs. Hosts are the load-bearing minority. Lose one and it cascades through everyone who attended their events. Growth doesn't stabilise communities, it stresses them: a referral spike floods the system with newcomers, and if host capacity can't absorb them, satisfaction drops and churn follows. The smallest topic dies first, not because it's small but because it can't sustain even one host. Platform curation keeps niches breathing; it can't make them thrive without an organiser. The design decisions that matter most aren't acquisition levers. They're the ones that protect the small group doing the actual work.

This isn't production research. The model isn't calibrated against real platform data. Slider defaults are educated, not measured, and I haven't run sensitivity analyses to validate emergent rates against the benchmarks it cites. But the point of a tool like this isn't prediction. It's helping a designer see causality as a shape rather than a list of factors. When I look at a community product now, I notice myself reaching for different questions. Who's hosting, what happens when they stop, how long does this thing have to absorb a referral spike. The simulation didn't tell me what to design. It changed what I look for.
