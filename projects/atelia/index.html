Atelia Product Design

Atelia is a conceptual digital shopping companion built around an AI virtual try-on. Users paste a product link from any store and see the garment on their own body. The design challenge wasn't building try-on, it was deciding where in the shopping journey it creates the most value.

Try-on is a feature, not a product. To make it useful, I needed to find the moment where shoppers are actively losing confidence but still have buying intent. Research pointed to the gap right before checkout, where 64% of users hesitate because they can't tell how an item will look on them. Positioning Atelia at that decision moment, rather than as a standalone gimmick, framed every subsequent design decision.

I ran 47 surveys and 5 interviews to understand how people actually shop across stores. The strongest pattern was scattered saving. People screenshot items into camera rolls, pin them, save them to wishlists across four-plus retailers on average, then can't find anything when they want to buy. They also organize by occasion (vacation, work, an event) rather than by product category. This shifted Atelia from a try-on tool into a unified place to gather, try on, and decide.

The product is structured around three behavioral modes that map to how people actually shop. BEFORE is collecting, the Pinterest-like mode of gathering inspiration. DURING is creating, where the user mixes items on a canvas and generates outfits. AFTER is reviewing, where kept looks get organized or sent to checkout. The navigation, vocabulary, and feature priority all derive from this frame. An Item is one garment, an Outfit is an AI-generated combination, a Collection is a user-made group, and Add is distinct from Save so first-time capture stays low-friction without committing the user to the archive.

I tested the prototype with 5 participants in moderated usability sessions. Two issues surfaced: poor History discoverability and hesitation around uploading body photos. Both drove follow-up changes, stronger History affordances and an on-demand privacy flow for model setup. The project was a solo design effort over six weeks, ending in an interactive Figma prototype.
